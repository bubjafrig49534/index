People have been saying messages and ideas spead like viruses for more than sixty years.

H.G. Landau and Anatol Raport were the first to compare the spread of information to the spread of diseases, in the 15th edition of the Bulletin of Mathematical Biophysics in 1953. They used equations to show how advanced models that help predict the spread of disease could also be applied to the spread of information.
Nearly a decade later, public health scholar Vaun Newill and scentist William Goffman published a paper expanding on this concept. They wrote:
People are susceptible to certain ideas and resistant to others. Once an individual is infected with an idea they may in turn, after some period of time, transmit it to others.
And it makes sense, right? You tell a friend about a cool new donut shop, they tell their friends, who then tell their friends. Pretty soon the shop has a line wrapping around the block every Saturday morning. The idea (that donut shop is good) spreads from person-to-person, just like the chicken pox virus spreads from person-to-person, until it infects an entire kindergarten class.

It seems obvious to us now, sixty years later. The word meme and the phrase going viral are parts of our everyday discourse. But it's worth stepping back and examining how we got here. Let's start with those real-world viruses.
You only need two conditions for a virus to spread:

 


exposure to infectious material

 

 


a population, which contains three types of people:

 

Infectives: host of the infectious material
Susceptibles: those likely to be infected when they make contact with the infectious material
The removed: not included due to immunity, hospitalization, or death
Let's take Darwin and his theory of evolution as an example of an idea that may have spread like a virus. We can say that Darwin was host to the infective (his theory of evolution). When he published The Origin of the Species in 1859, he was spreading the infective to other scientists. The scientists who saw the value of his theory and incorporated it into their worldview were susceptibles. The Catholic church failed to accept evolution for decades. Since they were initially immune to Darwin's idea, they can be considered removed.

Who cares if ideas spread like viruses or not?

The reason we should pay attention to this analogy is simple. Everyone wants to figure out how to get their big idea to catch on. Once you establish that ideas spread from person-to-person, you can use models from epidemiology to predict how your idea might spread through culture.

The internet renewed interest in the concept of messages-as-viruses

In the Media Virus (1994), author Douglas Rushkoff insists that "this term is not being used as a metaphor. These media events are not like viruses. They are viruses. They spread rapidly if they provoke our interest, and their success is dependent on the particular strengths and weaknesses of the host organism, popular culture. In his 2007 book The Tipping Point, Malcolm Gladwell said the "best way to understand the emergence of fashion trends, the ebb and flow of crime waves, or the rise of teenage smoking, or any number of the other mysterious changes that mark everyday life is to think of them as epidemics.
"Ideas and products and messages and behaviors spread just like viruses do."
And in Unleashing the Ideavirus (2000), Seth Godin breaks down the analogy even further, saying that sneezers "infect the masses" with contagious ideas that spread far and wide, passing from person-to-person.

"The longer people are sneezing about your idea," Seth writes, "the more people they infect. Ideas not only replicate easily and well, they get more powerful and more valuable as you deliver them to more people."

But do ideas really spread like viruses?

Going viral is only one way to explain the diffusion of messages and ideas. It's a model, an "idealization of the real situation in which the complex process is reduced to its essential properties" (source: Generalization of epidemic theory).

Is virality the best model? If it is, how does knowing that help us create ideas that catch on?

The short answer: I don't know. That's why I created this website. To build it, I talked to people from different disciplines to find out how they think ideas spread in their respective industries.

I learned something unexpected in each interview, and I think you will, too.
